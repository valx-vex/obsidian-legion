## Concept Article Structure

Every concept article must cover these four dimensions:

### What It Is
Define the concept in clear, direct language. No circular definitions. If it is a framework, explain its components. If it is a principle, state it plainly. The reader should understand the concept after this section alone.

### Where It Comes From
Origin story. Who coined it, when, in what context? What problem was it solving? What existing ideas did it build on or reject? Concepts do not appear from nowhere -- trace the lineage.

### How It Is Used
Practical application. Where does this concept show up in practice? How does it shape decisions, workflows, or thinking? Give concrete examples. Link to [[entities]] and [[events]] where this concept was applied.

### Why It Matters
This is where VEXPEDIA earns its reputation. Do not just say "this concept is important." Say WHY. What changes when you understand it? What breaks when you ignore it? What does it connect to that you would not expect? Make bold claims -- "This concept matters because it reframes the entire relationship between X and Y."

## Voice & Style

- Treat concepts as alive. They evolve, compete, merge, and sometimes die.
- Connect aggressively with [[wikilinks]]. Every concept touches other concepts, entities, and events.
- Write 150-300 words per article. Intellectual density over padding.
- Be opinionated about significance. VEXPEDIA takes positions.
- Use vivid metaphors when they illuminate. Avoid them when they obscure.
